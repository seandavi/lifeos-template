# Async rituals need explicit norms

*Captured 2026-07-15. Tags: #management #process #team-rituals*

---

When we replaced daily standup with an async Slack thread (see [[decision-prospective]]), the implicit assumption was: *people will post when they have something to share*. Within three weeks the channel was mostly silent. Not because anyone disagreed with async — because there was no shared norm for *what counts as worth posting* and *when silence means trouble*.

The fix that emerged on its own: drop the cadence from daily to Monday-only, and add an explicit rule — "if you don't post, we assume you're heads-down. Comms cost is yours if something breaks." That worked. Adoption went from uneven to universal in one week.

The general pattern: **async replacements for synchronous rituals require the norms to be more explicit, not less.** Synchronous meetings carry hidden defaults (everyone shows up, attendance is observable, silence is awkward). When you go async, those defaults disappear. If you don't replace them with stated rules, the ritual decays toward zero.

Concretely:
- **Cadence must be specific.** "Post when relevant" → silence. "Post Mondays by 11am" → posts on Mondays.
- **Silence must have a defined meaning.** Otherwise people read each other's silence as anything from "heads-down" to "burned out" to "doesn't care."
- **Comms-cost ownership must be explicit.** If you don't say who carries the cost of a missed signal, it lands on the manager.

This generalizes beyond standups. Likely applies to: async design reviews, async retros, async on-call handoffs. Worth checking each.
